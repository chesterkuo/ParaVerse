AI and Workforce Displacement: Policy Options for Hong Kong
Labour and Welfare Bureau Research Paper | January 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Artificial intelligence adoption in Hong Kong's service-oriented economy is projected to automate 23-34% of current job tasks by 2030. This paper examines the workforce displacement risk, identifies most-affected sectors, and evaluates policy interventions to manage the transition. The key recommendation is a comprehensive AI Workforce Transition Programme combining reskilling subsidies, an AI deployment tax, and a portable benefits system for gig workers.

CURRENT STATE OF AI ADOPTION IN HONG KONG

A survey of 2,400 Hong Kong enterprises conducted in Q4 2025 reveals:
- 42% have deployed AI in at least one business function (up from 18% in 2023)
- 68% of large enterprises (>500 employees) use AI for customer service automation
- 31% of SMEs have adopted AI tools for accounting, marketing, or operations
- Top AI applications: Customer service chatbots (52%), document processing (44%), predictive analytics (38%), code generation (29%), content creation (25%)

SECTOR-BY-SECTOR DISPLACEMENT RISK

1. Financial Services (520,000 workers)
Risk Level: HIGH
- Back-office operations (settlements, compliance checking): 45% automation potential
- Relationship managers: 15% automation potential (AI-augmented, not replaced)
- Insurance claims processing: 60% automation potential
- Estimated job displacement by 2030: 35,000-52,000 positions
- New roles created: AI prompt engineers, algorithm auditors, human-AI collaboration specialists
- Net impact: -20,000 to -30,000 jobs

2. Retail and Hospitality (650,000 workers)
Risk Level: HIGH
- Cashier and checkout: 70% automation potential (self-service, computer vision)
- Inventory management: 55% automation potential
- Hotel front desk: 40% automation potential
- Food preparation (fast food): 30% automation potential
- Estimated displacement: 45,000-65,000 positions
- New roles: Experience designers, robotics technicians, digital marketing specialists
- Net impact: -25,000 to -40,000 jobs

3. Professional Services (280,000 workers)
Risk Level: MEDIUM-HIGH
- Legal research and document review: 50% automation potential
- Accounting and audit (routine tasks): 55% automation potential
- Architecture and engineering drafting: 35% automation potential
- Medical diagnostics (radiology, pathology): 25% automation potential
- Estimated displacement: 18,000-28,000 positions
- New roles: AI-assisted lawyers, data-driven consultants
- Net impact: -8,000 to -15,000 jobs

4. Transport and Logistics (180,000 workers)
Risk Level: MEDIUM
- Warehouse operations: 45% automation potential
- Last-mile delivery: 20% automation potential (regulatory barriers to autonomous vehicles)
- Dispatching and route planning: 60% automation potential
- Estimated displacement: 12,000-20,000 positions
- Net impact: -5,000 to -12,000 jobs

5. Education (120,000 workers)
Risk Level: LOW-MEDIUM
- Administrative tasks: 35% automation potential
- Tutoring and assessment: 20% automation potential (AI tutors supplementing, not replacing)
- Teaching: 5% automation potential (high human interaction requirement)
- Estimated displacement: 4,000-8,000 positions
- Net impact: -1,000 to -3,000 jobs

TOTAL PROJECTED DISPLACEMENT

Conservative scenario: 60,000-85,000 net job losses by 2030
Moderate scenario: 85,000-120,000 net job losses by 2030
Aggressive scenario: 120,000-160,000 net job losses by 2030

Hong Kong's current unemployment rate: 2.9% (Q4 2025)
Projected unemployment impact: +1.5 to +4.2 percentage points without intervention

DEMOGRAPHIC VULNERABILITY

Most vulnerable groups:
- Workers aged 45-60 with secondary education only: 340,000 people
- Administrative and clerical workers: 280,000 people
- Part-time and gig economy workers without employment protection: 190,000 people
- New immigrants with limited Cantonese proficiency: 85,000 people

Least vulnerable groups:
- STEM graduates under 35
- Healthcare professionals (nursing, therapy)
- Creative professionals (design, media)
- Skilled trades (electricians, plumbers — physical + cognitive)

POLICY OPTIONS EVALUATED

Option A: AI Deployment Tax
- Levy of 1-3% on corporate AI spending above HKD 500,000/year
- Estimated revenue: HKD 2.8-4.5 billion/year
- Pros: Creates transition funding, slows displacement pace
- Cons: May reduce Hong Kong's competitiveness as AI hub, difficult to define "AI spending"
- International precedents: South Korea's robot tax (2018), EU proposed AI levy (2025)

Option B: Universal Reskilling Subsidy
- HKD 30,000 per worker for approved reskilling programmes
- Target: 200,000 workers over 5 years
- Estimated cost: HKD 6 billion total
- Pros: Directly addresses displacement, individual empowerment
- Cons: Programme quality varies, some workers unable to reskill
- International precedent: Singapore's SkillsFuture programme

Option C: Portable Benefits System
- Decouple healthcare, pension, and training benefits from employment
- Workers carry benefits across jobs, gig work, and career transitions
- Estimated cost: HKD 1.2 billion/year in administrative setup
- Pros: Supports gig economy workers, reduces job lock
- Cons: Complex implementation, employer resistance

Option D: AI Transparency and Transition Notice
- Require companies to provide 6-month notice before AI-driven layoffs of >20 workers
- Mandatory transition support plans including reskilling and placement assistance
- Estimated cost to employers: HKD 15,000-30,000 per affected worker
- Pros: Orderly transitions, employer accountability
- Cons: May incentivize gradual replacement to avoid threshold

RECOMMENDED POLICY PACKAGE

The research team recommends a combined approach:
1. Implement Option B (Universal Reskilling Subsidy) as the centrepiece — HKD 6B over 5 years
2. Implement Option D (AI Transition Notice) for companies >100 employees
3. Pilot Option C (Portable Benefits) in the gig economy sector
4. Study Option A (AI Tax) for potential Phase 2 implementation in 2029

Funding sources:
- General revenue: HKD 3 billion
- Employer contributions: HKD 2 billion (via Enhanced MPF mechanism)
- Innovation and Technology Fund reallocation: HKD 1 billion

STAKEHOLDER POSITIONS

Business Community (HKGCC, FHKI):
- Strongly oppose AI tax
- Support reskilling subsidies (prefer voluntary participation)
- Oppose mandatory transition notices
- Argue: "Hong Kong must remain competitive; over-regulation will push AI investment to Singapore and Shenzhen"

Labour Unions (HKCTU, FLU):
- Support AI tax and mandatory transition notices
- Demand stronger safety nets and unemployment insurance
- Push for worker representation in AI deployment decisions
- Argue: "Workers bear the cost of automation while shareholders capture the benefits"

Education Sector:
- Support reskilling subsidies (creates demand for their programmes)
- Universities expanding AI-related courses: 45% more seats in 2026
- VTC and HKDI developing micro-credential programmes for mid-career switchers

Technology Industry:
- Oppose any regulation that slows AI adoption
- Willing to fund industry-specific training programmes
- Prefer self-regulation to government mandates
- Some companies (HSBC, Cathay, MTR) already running internal reskilling programmes

Young Workers (Under 30):
- Generally optimistic about AI as a career enhancer
- 72% believe AI will create more opportunities than it destroys
- Demand better access to AI training and tools
- Concerned about housing affordability, not AI displacement
